Patterns

A few things hiding in the data.

Survivorship Bias

The 1980s are 56% starred. The 2020s, 11%. This isn't because the eighties produced better books—it's because a book from 1986 only makes it onto this list if it survived nearly four decades of cultural filtering. Every old book here has already proven itself. Every new book is still on probation.

The Australian Shelf

50% of the Australian books are starred, against 19% for the rest. Four of the five starred Australian titles cluster in the late 1980s and early 1990s—the books a child in Australia would have grown up with. The list-maker's childhood is showing.

10 of 117 books are by Australian creators—8% of the list, but 20% of the stars.

Who Makes the Books

In collaborations, women are credited as illustrator twice as often as men (27 to 12). The “man writes, woman draws” arrangement outnumbers the reverse almost 3 to 1 (16 books to 6). But the picture is changing: in the 2020s, half the books are by women working alone.

The Repeat Visitors

Carson Ellis appears three times—once as author and twice as illustrator, bridging Susan Cooper and Mac Barnett into this collection. Kyo Maclear and Gracey Zhang are the only author–illustrator duo to collaborate twice. Illustrators are the connective tissue of children's literature.

Where the Stars Are

You don’t read Meditations or The Hitchhiker’s Guide to a baby unless you think they’re great. The chapter books and poetry books are pre-filtered by the decision to read them at all. Stars would be meaningless.

All 25 stars are in the picture books section—the only section large enough for starring to mean anything. With 106 picture books, a star picks out the 24% that stood apart. With 8 chapter books and 3 poetry books, every one was chosen deliberately. Starring all of them would say nothing.
